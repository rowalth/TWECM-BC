Never use YOU!

Tip: Saying The Word "You" Often Starts Fights And Makes People MadThink about all the myriad ways that this 3-letter word "you" gets used in conversations and emails:I need to talk to you…Why didn’t you consider those options?You’ve got some big challenges ahead of you..What were you thinking?The expectation is that you show up to the meeting with a completed agenda…In each of those cases, the word ‘you’ has a distinctly accusatory undertone. Do those examples generate a warm fuzzy feeling? Or a feeling of being attacked, judged and set-up for a deeply unpleasant conversation? Most people would say it’s the latter.At an unconscious level, we know the word ‘you’ is pretty touchy. It’s so well known, in fact, that much of the time when people use the word ‘you’ in a pleasant way, we actually change the word into something like ‘ya.’For example, we don’t often walk up to a friend and say “what are you doing?” because that feels accusatory. Instead we say something like “whatcha doin?” or “whatcha up to?” or even “’sup?” We do this because when we articulate the word “you” out loud, it’s really tough to do it in a way that doesn’t feel like an attack or interrogation.Another reason the word ”you” is so problematic is that there are often a lot of assumptions and interpretations hidden in the subsequent words. For example, if I say “you didn’t pay attention to what I said yesterday at the meeting and that’s why this report isn’t correct” I’m making a lot of assumptions. Not only am I accusing the other person of not paying attention (which may or may not be true), I’m making a really big assumption that the reason why the report is wrong is their inattention. Maybe the report is wrong because of an honest mistake. Maybe a chart got cut off. Maybe they listened intently but misinterpreted what I said. And maybe I wasn’t anywhere near as clear in my instructions as I thought I was. It’s tough to say the word “you” and not have all the words that follow come off as accusatory and loaded. And perhaps worst of all, it puts us at risk of being horribly wrong.
